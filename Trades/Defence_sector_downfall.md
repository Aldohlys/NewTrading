# European Defence Sector Downfall — March 2026

## Context
European defence stocks dropped sharply in late March 2026, despite the Iran war (Operation Epic Fury) starting in early March. This note investigates the paradox.

---

## Why European Defence Stocks Dropped

### 1. Valuations ran too far ahead of earnings
The sector had a massive multi-year rally (Rheinmetall from ~200 to 1000+ EUR). By early 2026, valuations were richer than tech stocks. When Rheinmetall reported on March 11, its 40-45% sales growth guidance was called "realistic but soft" by Jefferies — the stock plunged 8% in one day. Leonardo hit records after beating, but the sector overall couldn't sustain the valuation premium. Investors now in "show me the earnings" mode.

### 2. Ukraine peace deal overhang
Trump signaled progress on a Russia-Ukraine peace deal. Since the original European rearmament thesis was built on the Ukraine war, any peace signal triggers mechanical selling. Rheinmetall, Saab, Leonardo all dropped, with the A&D index logging its biggest weekly loss. JPMorgan called it a "compelling entry point," arguing peace would actually mean *more* European spending — but the market sold first.

### 3. Classic "buy the rumour, sell the news" on Iran
Defence stocks spiked on the initial Iran strikes (March 1-2), then faded. Well-documented pattern: stocks spike on first evidence of conflict, then dip as war gets priced in. US defence names (LMT, NOC, RTX) captured most of the Iran uplift since it's a US-led operation — European contractors are less directly involved.

### 4. Iran war actually hurts risk appetite broadly
Oil above $100/barrel, broader equity selloffs, and uncertainty hit European markets hard. The defence sector, already crowded and expensive, became a source of funds for rotation into energy and commodities.

### 5. Earnings proof gap (Bloomberg)
The multi-year rally was built on spending *commitments* — but actual contract deliveries, margins, and cash flows haven't caught up to where valuations are.

## Safran (SAF) — Case Study

### Price action
- Down ~19% in 30 days, from ~350 EUR peak to ~281 EUR (March 26, 2026)
- Trading at 92% premium to estimated fair value
- YTD down 5.3% despite being up 28% over the past 12 months
- 52-week range: 190.70 - 350.80 EUR
- Analyst consensus: Buy, avg target 358 EUR (low 257, high 416)

### Why SAF gets hit from both sides

**Not an Iran play.** Revenue is ~75% civil aerospace (LEAP engines for A320neo/737 MAX). The Iran war doesn't generate LEAP orders — it benefits missile/munitions makers (Rheinmetall, MBDA, Lockheed). SAF gets the defence sector sell-off without the war upside.

**737 MAX overhang.** Boeing's MAX delivery uncertainty directly impacts LEAP engine volumes and breakeven targets. Civil aviation headwind, not a defence tailwind.

**Oil above $100 hurts airlines.** Iran conflict -> oil spike -> airline profitability pressure -> risk to aftermarket engine maintenance demand (Safran's most profitable segment). The war actually *hurts* SAF's core business.

**Indiscriminate sector rotation.** When funds de-risk European A&D as a basket (ETFs, sector indices), Safran sells off with Rheinmetall and Thales regardless of its different business mix.

### Trading angle
For a potential BOT entry, SAF would need the selling to stabilize first. At 281 EUR it's approaching the lower end of analyst targets (257 EUR low). Need to see a base form and sector rotation exhaustion before looking for a breakout signal. The aftermarket engine business is genuinely high-quality — if it overshoots to the downside, could become interesting.

---

## Key Takeaways
- European defence is a crowded, overvalued trade unwinding on multiple catalysts
- Iran war benefits US primes, not European A&D broadly
- Safran is collateral damage — civil aero business actually hurt by oil spike
- Watch for base formation before considering entries

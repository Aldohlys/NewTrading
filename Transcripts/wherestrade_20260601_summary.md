## Summary

Patrick Ceresna recorded this "Where's the Trade?" macro roadmap live from the Lisbon Mastermind, deliberately compressed to ~30 minutes (no tutorials scheduled this week; trades to be published live with the in-room group, breakout trade tomorrow). The session is dominated by one thesis — an impending oil-supply / physical-inventory inflection — and the intermarket consequences that radiate out of it.

**Oil supply / inventory crisis — the central thesis.** Over the weekend Iran broke off talks and resumed missile strikes; the "rosy peace deal" Trump promised is not materializing, and crude reversed sharply higher (up ~\$6). Patrick used Polymarket odds as a framework for what informed-money bettors actually expect:
- Permanent peace deal by July 31 = **38%** (≈ two-out-of-three chance there is *no* deal by mid-summer).
- Strait of Hormuz traffic back to normal by end-June = **25%**.
- U.S. blockade lifted by June 30 = **45%**.
- Hormuz traffic returning by June 15 (two weeks) = **4%**.
The read: a near-term peace deal is "basically not realistic." He reiterated last week's point that at a Bernstein conference the **Chevron CEO and a senior Exxon executive** said the industry is "weeks away from reaching critical inventory levels" where demand destruction *has* to happen to recalibrate monstrous inventory depletion. He cited a chart from **Rory (commodity context)** of **Japanese crude oil inventories** showing the largest collapse/drawdown ever recorded. The paradox he keeps returning to: physical markets are screaming while paper/cash oil markets stay calm. He believes June will be "talked about for a very long time" — an inflection point where physical inventories and physical consumption cross with paper markets that have anchored on the wrong fair value.

He raised (without endorsing) the conspiracy view that futures are being actively manipulated lower to keep oil contained — and noted the self-defeating mechanic: artificially low prices prevent demand destruction and deplete inventories faster, worsening the eventual crisis, and once you've taken the situation "to the brink" the Iranians "have no reason to be your solution." His macro analogy is **COVID**: a *physical* problem (people physically sick, economies physically shutting) versus the *paper* markets that stocks trade on — Larry Kudlow reassuring everyone "everything is fine until it wasn't," then a violent freak-out. Today the market only cares about making "stupid amounts of money on AI," ignoring oil because oil "hasn't done anything." His scenario: AI reaches an **exhaustion point**, and if oil is rising at the same time you get a "monstrous narrative shift in the blink of an eye." The open question is *when*, and whether the inflection began today.

**Portfolio insurance is shockingly cheap — a structural vulnerability.** Skew is very low, put demand is absent, nobody is worried about hedging downside. Patrick frames the *complacency itself* as the risk. Contrast with the Feb/March sell-off: it was so telegraphed that everybody was prepared — the "crowded bar where someone yells fire but everyone's already positioned, so no trampling." Today the market has convinced itself nothing can go wrong because of how much money is being made. His prescription: even if you refuse to sell your longs, it is so cheap now to buy downside insurance that you should hold minimum portfolio protection — a volatility dampener embedded in the book that gives staying power and the ability to manage through short-term volatility.

**Crude oil technicals.** Up ~\$6, a reversal to the upside on the weekend escalation. The **\$100 area is the key threshold** — the Fib zone sits ~\$98 but he likes the simplicity of the \$100 round number. Above \$100 price clears trendlines, moving averages and Fib zones simultaneously, and clearing the **May high** is the "oh shit, something really is happening" trigger that could start a negative feedback loop. "We could be there tomorrow" if the inventory story becomes *the* narrative — then it's a race to be in the story first.

**Rates / bonds.** An early warning sign: SOFR futures (transcript renders this as "**sofa Futures**" — ASR error) never bought into the oil-collapse story — a "pathetic slagging formation," no Fibonacci retrace, no 50-day test, continuing to bake in inflation expectations. With oil reversing up, rates rising is the natural reaction function. The long bond has turned back down (i.e. yields up); **TLT** is down ~\$1 off a failure at the 50-day MA. Both 10-year and 30-year are failing Fib zones and the 50-day → another round of bond selling possible. **Longer-term, though, he flagged an "absolutely epic bond-buying opportunity" in the summer / into Q3**: the freak-out will wash out rate markets, oil higher triggers a global recession, and they'll be forced to ease. Right now everyone prioritizes inflation fears over recession fears; the pivot to recession fears is where the bond opportunity sits — "but right now you can't touch it."

**FX.** The U.S. dollar is trying to turn back up / advancing. The **euro** is edging toward **116** — "the edge of the cliff," his line in the sand. **Pound sterling** shows a flagging formation failing at the Fib zone / 50-day and rolling over. The **Japanese yen** is the pins-and-needles one: he's played the intervention story, but Japan is highly vulnerable to oil and food import prices — if those skyrocket the outlet may have to be the currency regardless of intervention. If the yen support line doesn't hold, that's a trade to pivot on quickly.

**Gold and gold miners.** Dollar up + oil up + bonds down → another gold breakdown setup. On an hourly basis the entire gold rally stalled at the **61.8% Fib**, and the little bounce failed at the 50-day MA. He doesn't want to "boogeyman" it, but you must be hedged: nobody should be leveraged / Delta-one long gold here. The **primary distribution cycle in gold is still underway**. **Gold miners** are down ~\$4; yesterday's bounce didn't even reach the 50% retrace before turning back down — measured moves point to a possible date with **\$75** on the downside. For long-dated **GLD / GDX (LEAP)** holders, the cleanest hedge is a **bear put spread overlay** to *dampen* downside volatility — the LEAPs already carry convexity, so you don't need to be Delta-one or outright long gamma on the downside; you just want enough dampening to do a tactical roll-down at better prices. His sequencing story: **bonds puke first → oil rips → stock markets react → recession kicks in → then the exact opposite** — they print money, cut rates, and gold enters a monstrous bull phase, though that may not arrive until the end of Q3. Plan for the next two-to-four months to be very challenging for gold while maintaining a 1–2-year bullish view; "put on your seatbelts" short-term.

**Other commodities.** **Copper** continues to not care, moving higher, looks fine. **Gasoline futures** are interesting — has *not* reacted the way crude has, a potential laggard that could catch up quickly on the upside. **Uranium** is "that boring" — long-term bull story intact, but short-to-intermediate term nobody's paying attention, no flows; be very patient. **Grains** (wheat, corn, soybean): if oil is turning up, this "could be the moment" they turn too, but no reaction yet — keep an eye on it.

**Asia / semis.** **Nikkei** and **KOSPI (Cospi)** continue to run hot; the measured move on KOSPI projects all the way to **10,000** — a parabolic blow-off he wasn't fully expecting but which looks clean. The big story is how much "gas in the tank" the **semiconductor index** has and when it stalls. His bubble dictum: "bubbles are never supposed to make sense — stupid prices go to stupid levels; if it made sense there'd be no such thing as a bubble. Stop using rational thought when talking bubbles."

**Latin America.** **Argentina** turned up and is bucking the trend, "marching to the beat of its own drum," independent of the others — interesting to see if it's starting something. **Brazil** is not puking but has clearly begun a distribution cycle. **Chile** bounced a little but is stuck in the mud. **Mexico** is trading sideways. Argentina is the lone one trying to turn up on its own.

**China — ending the turnaround trade.** China reversed off its high and is muddling along; this was the "line in the sand." They were positioned for a China turn, but it met all the criteria of stalling at a Fib zone and breaking back down — it "wants to make another full measured move" lower. He's **ending the China turnaround story / trade**. Offshore China stocks haven't moved like the onshore ones — the **China A50** is clearly disconnected from the attempt to bull higher. Bottom line: no flows are going into China, and if it doesn't immediately reverse and turn bullish there's no reason to stay in it.

**Breakout review (single names).**

- **Microsoft** screaming higher — this is the **profit-taking zone**, the Fib zone of the entire move, where "the magic button is most actively used"; measured moves are right here. Congratulations to those in it (implicit: take profits).
- **Visa** tried to turn up; the **50-day MA** is the important level — either this was a retracement and it goes higher, or it gets dragged down with consumer spending. **Walmart** "ruined the party" by confirming the **K-shaped economy** and a consumer in big trouble; Walmart's decline dragged **Costco** down and **McDonald's** has been "eating it" too — the bottom-end consumer is suffering. The question is whether that bleeds into the card networks like **Visa**. Visa has no credit risk (unlike **American Express**) — it's more of a service/fee name — but a failure here would ruin the trade.
- **Phillips 66** is clearly turning up, attempting a bull breakout, helped by oil turning back up. He "still loves this trade a lot" and wants to be in; it could arguably be a long-term position, but they're **breakout-trading it and will follow breakout-trader rules**. Energy plays broadly are "super interesting" right now.

**Q&A.**
- **S&P straddle / calendar straddle idea:** he doesn't hate it. The **VIX is very low**, so there's not much Vega risk → you can "100% be using calendar straddles here." Maybe Wednesday they'll look deeper at what they're priced at. Downside vol is simply not in demand — cheap, skew very low, "no one's worried about nothing" — a great time to buy downside. On **straddle vs strangle**: "literally the same thing"; strangles are popular because of the smaller capital outlay (attractive to smaller traders counting percentage on outlay), but behavior is the same as a straddle *so long as you don't hold into expiration*.
- **U.S. dollar (Gary):** the natural intermarket is for the dollar to go **higher** during this period; governments try to manipulate, but global flows matter and many economies have problems. If gold goes ripping, the dollar is a safe haven "no matter how much Trump doesn't want a stronger dollar."
- **FXI — cut or hold?** He doesn't see the **FXI** trade working anymore and would **not** put on new positions. His failure point was a break to a **lower low**, and it's "toying with it right here" — on a knife's edge in terms of closing it. If you want nothing to do with it, you can kill it; he would definitely not open new trades on it, especially newer members.

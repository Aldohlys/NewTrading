What is a "Pip"?
Pip stands for "Percentage in Point" or "Price Interest Point" - it's the smallest standardized price movement in a currency exchange rate.
General Definition

Standard pip: The fourth decimal place in most currency pairs (0.0001)
Exception: Japanese yen pairs use the second decimal place (0.01) as one pip
Fractional pips: Some brokers quote to 5th decimal (called "pipettes")

Forex Trading Context
Currency Pair Examples:

EUR/USD: From 1.1000 to 1.1001 = 1 pip move
GBP/USD: From 1.2500 to 1.2485 = 15 pip move
USD/JPY: From 110.00 to 110.05 = 5 pip move
USD/CHF: From 0.9000 to 0.8950 = 50 pip move

Pip Value Calculation:
Pip Value = (1 pip / Exchange Rate) × Lot Size
Example for EUR/USD:

Standard lot (100,000 units): 1 pip = $10
Mini lot (10,000 units): 1 pip = $1
Micro lot (1,000 units): 1 pip = $0.10

Futures Trading Context
6E (Euro FX Futures):

Contract size: €125,000
Minimum tick: 0.0001 (1 pip)
Tick value: $12.50 per pip
Example: 6E moves from 1.1000 to 1.1010 = 10 pips = $125 profit/loss

MSF (Micro Swiss Franc Futures):

Contract size: CHF 12,500 (1/10th of standard 6S)
Minimum tick: 0.0001 (1 pip)
Tick value: $1.25 per pip
Example: MSF moves from 0.9000 to 0.8950 = 50 pips = $62.50 profit/loss

Key Differences Futures vs Forex:

Standardized contracts: Futures have fixed contract sizes
Centralized exchange: Futures trade on CME vs decentralized forex
Margin requirements: Often lower for futures
Tick values: Fixed dollar amounts per pip in futures

Practical Trading Examples
Risk Management:

Stop loss: "I'll risk 20 pips on this trade"
Profit target: "Looking for 60 pips upside"
Position sizing: "With $1.25 per pip (MSF), 20 pip risk = $25 max loss"

Volatility Measurement:

Low volatility: 10-30 pips daily range
Medium volatility: 30-60 pips daily range
High volatility: 80+ pips daily range
